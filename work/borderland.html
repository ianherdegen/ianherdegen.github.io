The challenge

Hosting a live tournament season isn’t a landing page. It’s recruiting people who’ve never heard of you, planning nights with a venue and an event manager, staying inside a real budget so you can afford to run again if something slips, and building software + props that don’t feel like a dorm project.

The games weren’t sit-down computer matches — they were interactive immersive player-to-player experiences in a real room, with a doors-open field phase on the projector board and people competing face to face. The ambition was specific: not “become the next streamed competition franchise.” Put out energy that if a serious company walked in, they’d respect the craft — while contestants still had a night that was weird, fun, and human. Over-corporate kills the room. Juvenile craft kills credibility. Borderland had to sit in the middle.

My approach

I owned the product and a lot of the season ops end to end — player surface, in-room games, admin, street recruitment — and planned tournament nights alongside an event manager so the calendar, capacity, and day-of reality stayed honest. Finance tracking wasn’t optional: spend had to leave room to rerun a night if we needed to. On the build side I shipped the games and ops stack and tools for fabricating new props / décor, so each venue night could look like a new chapter instead of the same folding chairs.

The constraint wasn’t a demo reel. It was whether people would play their way in — 162 cleared the entry games and joined — and whether 160+ would walk into five Detroit venues because the invites, planning, budget, tournament access, SMS, games, and physical craft all held.

How the system works

One cloneable stack for the whole season: React + Vite on Vercel; Socket.IO live rooms (Render workers) powering the mobile game clients; Stripe for tournament access (visas); Twilio + segmented email for reminders; Turso behind Brain, Finance, and The Stream. Swap the venue décor, keep the product — Clubs @ Chroma through Finale @ Russell (May–June 2026).

The product story

Player path first — how someone found Borderland, bought tournament access, showed up, played, and left with people they met — then behind the scenes: planning, budget, props, and the ops stack that kept the season runnable without sanding the fun off or chasing franchise-scale production fantasies.

Player path

01 · Recruit

Play your way in — then you’re part of it

Goal: filter curiosity from intent; grow a real community, not a mailing list

162 people cleared the high-friction entry: roll a four-face die, win the matching mini-game (one of four suits), then unlock the interest form. That wasn’t a soft signup — they already played to get in, so they were part of Borderland even if they never made every arena night. Of those 162, 151 left a phone number voluntarily (optional field — 93%). Street cards and Movement Festival promo pulled the same community into rooms: 160+ showed up across five venues.

02 · Access

Tournament access we called “visas”

Goal: paid entry entitlements the night’s phases and games can trust

In Borderland, a visa was the product name for tournament access — not immigration paperwork. Stripe checkout wasn’t a tip jar. Buying a visa wrote into capacity rules — play vs watch, night caps, cutoffs — and those entitlements gated field-objective claims and player-to-player games. Scarcity and access had to be the same system, or day-of ops falls back to spreadsheets.

03 · Show-up

SMS, voice, and email that fill chairs

Goal: get registered players into the building

Admin ran Twilio SMS and voice/voicemail (including machine detection for voicemail drops) plus 29 templated email variants keyed to tournament night, timing, and registration state — same reminder stack every night, not a one-off Mailchimp scramble. Reminding people is unglamorous. Not doing it empties a venue.

04 · Opening phase

Doors open. Clock starts. The board is live.

Goal: immerse players the second they walk in

Nights ran on a timer like a live sports game — phases kicked off on the dot as doors opened, so arriving players dropped straight into the experience instead of milling around. First phase: field objectives across the venue and gallery — claim them, watch the projector scoreboard update, see who’s in the lead, earn advantages into the player-to-player games that followed.

05 · In-room games

Mobile Socket games. Admin on the clock.

Goal: sports-tight pacing with mid-game recovery

4 of 5 interactive immersive player-to-player games ran through a Socket.IO mobile app — venue setup was mostly décor and a projector, not a custom AV build each night. Admin controls handled pause, reset, and phase changes; when something broke mid-game, mobile agents let us correct state live without killing the room’s energy. Same cloneable stack night to night — change the gallery, keep the product.

06 · Community

Friendships that outlast one night

Goal: the night becomes a network

The point wasn’t a one-off crowd. Players met crew, came back when they could, and kept talking after doors closed. Discord Relay (“The Stream”) pushed that community into a public channel so the season wasn’t trapped in private threads — still fun, not a corporate offsite.

Behind the scenes

07 · Plan

Tournament nights with an event manager

Goal: nights that can actually run

Software doesn’t book a room. I planned the season calendar and day-of shape with an event manager — venues from Chroma through Russell, capacity, timing, and what “ready” meant before doors opened. Access entitlements and admin had to match that plan: play vs watch, night caps, cutoffs — so the product and the production weren’t two different stories.

08 · Budget

Spend lean enough to run again

Goal: headroom to rerun a night if needed

Live seasons die when the first slip empties the account. Finance tracked spend with a preview-before-apply assistant so money stayed visible — venues, prizes, props, Twilio, print — without turning the crew into accountants. The point wasn’t austerity theater. It was keeping enough runway that if a tournament night needed a redo, we could still put it on.

09 · Props & décor

Interfaces that fabricate something new

Goal: each tournament night can decorate without starting from scratch

Clubs needed a physical airship. I built the gore modeler — hull parameters in, fabricatable PDF patterns out — so the prop could be cut and assembled instead of redrawn by hand. Giant card tools did the same for venue sculptures. That’s the craft bar: not a stock backdrop. Software that makes it cheaper and faster to put something new in the room.

10 · Ops stack

Brain, agent, and Call for Crew

Goal: run the season without living in DMs

Beacon Brain is a Turso-backed ops wiki — meeting notes, runbooks, workbooks — Notion-shaped for how we actually ran venues. I wired a Cursor / MCP-style agent against Brain so notes and follow-ups could be drafted from the knowledge base instead of disappearing in chat. Call for Crew recruited gamemakers and builders so each night had people behind the curtain.

Reflection

Experiential products fail when recruitment, planning, money, software, and the street live in separate brains. Borderland worked because those pieces pulled together — and because the goal was a room full of people who met each other, not a slide about becoming the next platform.

We weren’t chasing a streamed competition franchise. We were chasing a standard: fun for contestants, disciplined enough to rerun a night, and crafted so that if a large company looked in, they’d see intentional work — not a gag. 162 people played past the entry gate and became part of Borderland; 151 left phones; 1,000 cards and festival promo helped bring 160+ into 5 venues and 5 interactive immersive player-to-player games. I built the stack, planned with an event manager, and ran the season.
