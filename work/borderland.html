The challenge

Hosting a live gaming season isn’t a landing page. It’s recruiting people who’ve never heard of you, planning nights with a venue and an event manager, staying inside a real budget so you can afford to run again if something slips, and building software + props that don’t feel like a dorm project.

The ambition was specific: not “become Netflix.” Put out energy that if a serious company walked in, they’d respect the craft — while contestants still had a night that was weird, fun, and human. Over-corporate kills the room. Juvenile craft kills credibility. Borderland had to sit in the middle.

My approach

I owned the product and a lot of the season ops end to end — player surface, live games, admin, street recruitment — and planned arenas alongside an event manager so the calendar, capacity, and day-of reality stayed honest. Finance tracking wasn’t optional: spend had to leave room to rerun a night if we needed to. On the build side I shipped the games and ops stack and tools for fabricating new props / décor, so each venue could look like a new chapter instead of the same folding chairs.

The constraint wasn’t a demo reel. It was whether 160+ people would walk into five Detroit venues because the invites, planning, budget, visas, SMS, games, and physical craft all held.

What shipped

  • Recruitment & community — 1,000 street cards, Movement Festival promo, suit path, returning-player + referral flows; friendships formed across nights
  • Event planning — multi-venue season calendar planned with an event manager; capacity and day-of ops wired into visas and admin
  • Budget discipline — Finance ledger so spend stayed visible and reruns stayed possible
  • Visas — Stripe checkout, capacity caps, arena reservation policy wired into access
  • Arena Hunt — physical QR / scan codes ↔ timed digital hunt with claim rules and bonuses into phase-2 games
  • Live games — Socket.IO multiplayer (Breakpoint, Echo, Fetch, Zombie Hunt + suite) with venue projectors and TTS callers
  • Show-up ops — Twilio SMS + voice/voicemail, 29 email templates, admin dashboard for day-of
  • Prop & décor tools — airship gore modeler → fabricatable PDF; giant card print tools so each arena could decorate something new
  • Satellites — Call for Crew, Beacon Brain (ops wiki + meetings), Finance, Discord Relay; Cursor / MCP-style agent against Brain

How the system works

React + Vite on Vercel; Socket.IO live rooms (Render workers); Stripe for visas; Twilio for SMS and voice; segmented email by arena timing and registration state; Turso behind Brain, Finance, and The Stream. Season ran Clubs @ Chroma through Finale @ Russell (May–June 2026).

The product story

Same loop as any serious product case: recruit demand, plan operations, take money honestly, keep budget headroom, ship the experience in the room, then keep the org runnable — without sanding the fun off or chasing Netflix scale.

01 · Recruit

Pull players from all over into a room

Goal: strangers → a Detroit season

Borderland started in the street and at Movement Festival — 1,000 invitation cards and festival promo into 160+ participants across five venues. Recruiting wasn’t a growth-hack deck; it was hand-to-hand: cards, conversations, suit paths, and a reason to trust a weird night. Friendships and crew connections formed across arenas. That was the outcome that mattered.

02 · Plan

Arenas with an event manager

Goal: nights that can actually run

Software doesn’t book a room. I planned the season calendar and day-of shape with an event manager — venues from Chroma through Russell, capacity, timing, and what “ready” meant before doors opened. Visas and admin had to match that plan: play vs watch, arena caps, cutoffs — so the product and the production weren’t two different stories.

03 · Visas

Payments that unlock the night

Goal: entitlements games and hunts can trust

Stripe visas weren’t a tip jar. Checkout wrote into capacity rules — play vs watch, arena caps, cutoffs — and those entitlements gated hunt claims and live games. Scarcity and access had to be the same system, or day-of ops falls back to spreadsheets.

04 · Budget

Spend lean enough to run again

Goal: headroom to rerun a night if needed

Live seasons die when the first slip empties the account. Finance tracked spend with a preview-before-apply assistant so money stayed visible — venues, prizes, props, Twilio, print — without turning the crew into accountants. The point wasn’t austerity theater. It was keeping enough runway that if an arena needed a redo, we could still put it on.

05 · Props & décor

Interfaces that fabricate something new

Goal: each arena can decorate without starting from scratch

Clubs needed a physical airship. I built the gore modeler — hull parameters in, fabricatable PDF patterns out — so the prop could be cut and assembled instead of redrawn by hand. Giant card tools did the same for hunt sculptures. That’s the craft bar: not a stock backdrop. Software that makes it cheaper and faster to put something new in the room.

06 · Arena Hunt

Physical codes ↔ digital claims

Goal: venue hunt that survives real crowds

Arena Hunt turned printed QR / scan codes into timed claims with caps and bonuses into phase-2 games. Physical props and digital state had to agree — or the night breaks the second someone scans twice.

07 · Live games

Fun for contestants. Serious under the hood.

Goal: earn respect without feeling corporate

Socket.IO rooms ran Breakpoint, Echo, Fetch, and Zombie Hunt with server authority — while venue projectors and TTS callers kept the whole room on one program. Players got a night that felt wild. The systems underneath had to feel intentional — the opposite of “juvenile app + folding tables.”

08 · Show-up ops

SMS, voice, and email that fill chairs

Goal: get registered players into the building

Admin ran Twilio SMS and voice/voicemail (including machine detection for voicemail drops) plus 29 templated email variants keyed to arena, timing, and registration state. Reminding people is unglamorous. Not doing it empties a venue.

09 · Brain + agent

Meeting notes that don’t vanish in chat

Goal: ops knowledge the season can actually use

Beacon Brain is a Turso-backed ops wiki — meeting notes, runbooks, workbooks — Notion-shaped for how we actually ran venues. I wired a Cursor / MCP-style agent against Brain so notes and follow-ups could be drafted from the knowledge base instead of disappearing in DMs.

10 · Community stream

Crew + Relay — keep the human parts

Goal: staff the season and keep community visible

Call for Crew recruited gamemakers and builders. Discord Relay (“The Stream”) pushed community into a public channel so the season wasn’t trapped in private threads. Satellites kept ops adult without turning the night into a corporate offsite — friendships first, systems in service of that.

Reflection

Experiential products fail when recruitment, planning, money, software, and the street live in separate brains. Borderland worked because those pieces pulled together — and because the goal was a room full of people who met each other, not a slide about becoming the next platform.

We weren’t chasing Netflix scale. We were chasing a standard: fun for contestants, disciplined enough to rerun a night, and crafted so that if a large company looked in, they’d see intentional work — not a gag. 1,000 cards and festival promo brought 160+ participants across 5 venues and 5 live games. I built the stack, planned with an event manager, and ran the season.
