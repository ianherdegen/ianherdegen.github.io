The challenge

Campus parking is scarce and fragmented. Students need a spot near campus; hosts and property managers have inventory — but one-off listings don’t scale when you’re running lots, semester leases, and game-day spikes.

Marketplaces don’t stall on “more pins on a map.” They stall when capacity is opaque, prices can be gamed at checkout, landlords don’t control who parks before money moves, and payouts can’t route to the right seller account. BuyMySpot had to work as a trustworthy two-sided market and evolve into SaaS operators could run inventory through.

My approach

As product co-owner I ran the roadmap end to end — prioritizing what got built based on customer needs and usage patterns, then reprioritizing as signal came in. I helped spearhead the B2B shift (bulk listing, waitlists, capacity calendars…) and pushed the conversion lever that made leases finish on-platform: in-app chat. Before that, there was no in-product thread — deals drifted to text/email or stalled. With chat on the listing, median time to a signed lease landed under 48 hours.

I directed the technical roadmap in partnership with outside developers — translating customer needs into specs, making the tradeoff calls, and keeping the platform’s services (ex. spots, leases, payments, price calculation, chat, notifications) healthy as usage grew. I drove the sales side primarily myself: persistently building the property-manager relationships that turned a student marketplace into a B2B product.

What shipped

  • Discovery — map + list find-a-spot across campus markets (Ann Arbor, East Lansing, Madison, State College+)
  • Capacity calendars — occupancy over date ranges from overlapping approved leases (seller app)
  • Pricing engine — daily / weekly / monthly / full-lease schemes, subscription vs upfront, proration; quotes sealed so checkout can’t rewrite the invoice
  • Lease gate — request → landlord approve/reject before payments clear
  • Stripe Connect — custom multi payout accounts per seller (Connect’s one-account limit wasn’t enough for multi-property PMs)
  • Chat — in-app messaging so deals close on the listing instead of drifting off-platform
  • White-label storefronts — shareable branded inventory URLs for property managers
  • Platform — GraphQL BFF over Users, Spots, Leases, Payments, Price Calculation, Chat, and Notifications (AWS Lambda + EventBridge)

How the system works

Dual frontends: a Next.js marketing and discovery layer, and a React product app on S3 + CloudFront. Domain microservices run as AWS Lambda with MongoDB Atlas per service, stitched through a GraphQL BFF. Auth sits on Cognito. Async work fans out over EventBridge and SQS — lease state, payment charges/transfers, chat notifications. Pricing lives in its own service. Money movement runs on Stripe Connect so hosts get paid without BuyMySpot becoming their bank.

Operator anecdote we kept because revenue concentration matters: one business generated nearly half of their game-day revenue from parking sales through the platform.

The product story

Five hard problems, one loop: find inventory, run it as capacity, price it honestly, pay the right bank account, and keep the deal on-platform in chat.

01 · Discovery

Students find a spot without bouncing

Goal: reduce time-to-find

Find-a-spot put map pins and a scrollable listing pane on the same screen. Students could scan by neighborhood, price, and amenities — the path from “I need parking near campus” to a concrete listing without a second results page.

02 · Capacity calendar

Lots run as capacity, not one-off listings

Goal: let property managers operate inventory at lot scale

B2B inventory isn’t “list every space as its own pin.” It’s property → spot type → capacity (numberOfSpaces), so a lot can sell N spaces over a date range. Capacity calendars turn overlapping approved leases into an occupancy view — what operators need before they overbook a spot.

03 · Pricing engine

Prices stay honest through checkout

Goal: one invoice engine for daily events and semester leases

The same inventory can be a game-day day-rate or an eight-month student lease. A dedicated pricing service builds the schedule (daily / weekly / monthly / full lease, subscription vs upfront, proration), then seals the quote so checkout can’t rewrite fees.

04 · Multi-account payouts

Multiple payout accounts per seller

Goal: match how property managers actually bank

Stripe Connect only supported one seller account per user. That broke real property managers — different properties often pay into different banks. I pushed a custom multi-account solution on top of Connect: sellers can attach multiple payout accounts, properties point at the right one, and leases freeze that routing when the deal is made so money still lands in the correct bank.

05 · In-app chat

Chat keeps the deal on the listing

Goal: stop leases from drifting off-platform

Before in-app chat, there wasn’t a first message in the product — buyers and lessors bounced to text or email, and a lot of deals never came back. Messaging put the conversation on the listing itself. Once closes happened in-app, median time to a signed lease was under 48 hours.

06 · White-label storefront

Property managers get a shareable storefront

Goal: B2B distribution without a second product

White-label owner profiles let a PM share a branded inventory URL — their logo, their lots — without standing up a separate site. That’s the SaaS edge of the marketplace: operators can send demand straight to their capacity.

Reflection

Marketplaces don’t stall on “more listings” — they stall on capacity, price integrity, approval, and payouts. We made lots operable as capacity, sealed invoices through checkout, gated money behind landlord approval, and built multi payout accounts on Stripe Connect when one seller account per user wasn’t enough — while in-app chat kept closes on the listing (median under 48 hours to signed) on a platform that generated >$100K for property managers and served 500+ customers.

I co-owned this one with a team, pre–vibe-coding — directing outside developers while running the roadmap and property-manager relationships. On later products (Sculpt Architect, Borderland) I built end to end solo. I stay hands-on enough to prototype and ship small changes myself, so discovery doesn’t wait on a sprint queue — while partnering with developers on the larger builds.
